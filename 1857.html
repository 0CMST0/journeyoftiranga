Indian Mutiny / Sepoy Mutiny / First War of Independence

Indian Mutiny, also called Sepoy Mutiny or First War of Independence, widespread but unsuccessful rebellion against British rule in India in 1857-59. Begun in Meerut by Indian troops (sepoys) in the service of the British East India Company, it spread to Delhi, Agra, Kanpur, and Lucknow. In India it is often called the First War of Independence.

On 29 March 1857 at Barrackpore, Sepoy Mangal Pandey of the 34th Bengal Native Infantry attacked his officers. When his comrades were ordered to restrain him, they refused, but they stopped short of joining him in open revolt. The Mutiny proper began at Meerut on 10 May 1857. Eighty-five members of the 3rd Bengal Light Cavalry, who had been jailed for refusing to use cartridges they believed to be at odds with their religion, were broken out of prison by their comrades.

They ransacked the nearby military station and killed any Europeans they could find. Eventually all 10 Bengal Light Cavalry Regiments and most of the 74 Bengal Native Infantry Regiments were affected. Some units were disarmed before they had the chance to mutiny. In other cases, British officers refused to believe their men would rebel until it was too late. Not everyone who mutinied took up arms against the British. Many simply went home and refused to assist either side.

Most important results of revolt 1857 are:
1. Queen Victoria initially proclaimed different types of issues and primarily referred to the Indian governmental act of 1858, which can primarily be abolished by the British East India Company and the Indian government was led by the British crown.
2. The new modern oriented office was primarily created by the British government to restructure the urban culture of the nation. The secretary of the state was initially created by the British government.
3. The actual power of the British governor-general was initially increased and the vital post of viceroy position was initially created to maintain the official administrative work properly. Lord Canning was the first-ever viceroy of India....
4. The “Doctrine of Lapse” destroyed the right of adoption modules that were primarily granted by the British government. However, the annexed regions were primarily retired to the Indian ruler.
5. The actual impact of the revolt was primarily considered to abolish the critical rule of the “British East India” company and primarily marked the beginning of the British government.
